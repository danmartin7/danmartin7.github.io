What Is Research

When you hear or see the word research, especially in school settings, you may be likely to associate that word with science, labs, and research methods. However, research is much more expansive and broader than bunsen burners and dissecting a frog. Taking pictures and screenshots are research. Collecting data about your writing process is research.

What Is Primary Research?

You've probably heard the term primary research right before or after you heard the term secondary research. You probably wondered what the differences are between these two types of research. Primary research is research you create or conduct. Interviews, surveys, observations, experiments, and discourse analysis are just some of the methods associated with primary research. When you think about primary and secondary research, remember that primary research is the research that you conduct yourself. If you were to conduct an interview with an expert for a project, you conducted primary research. If you published the interview for a journal article, it will become a secondary source. Historian might rely on the examination and analysis of primary documents for a project. Examining a collection of newspapers from 1900 to answer a question about how newspapers structured content would make the newspapers a primary source.

What Is Secondary Research?

Secondary research is the type of research you are most likely familiar with. This is the type of research you've done in secondary school at the library, locating sources (books, articles, magazines, etc.) and referencing content from those sources to support a point or thesis in a paper. Locating someone's publication and citing it in your project is an example of secondary research.

What Is Qualitative Research?

You've most likley heard the terms qualitative and quantiative research before and wondered how they differ. Qualitative research includes methods of research that rely on interviews, case studies, observations, and surveys.
